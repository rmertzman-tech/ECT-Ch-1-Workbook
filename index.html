1. Arrive

Start with the chapter move

In Chapter 2, you learned that good intentions do not automatically scale into good patterns. A group, classroom, workplace, or institution may have a written rule that sounds responsible while a different lived rule quietly trains people to act otherwise.

Written rule: the official policy, instruction, or stated expectation.
Lived rule: what people learn is actually rewarded, punished, expected, or ignored.
Pattern repair: changing a rule, route, incentive, expectation, or practice so the same harmful pattern is less likely to repeat.

Privacy and example choice

You may use the provided case, a fictional example, a public example, a historical example, a composite example, or a low-stakes personal example. Do not disclose private, identifying, or sensitive information.

2. Read the Case

The Silent Safety Rule

A company handbook says that workers should report safety concerns immediately. The written rule is clear: if something looks unsafe, speak up.

But over time, workers learn a different lived rule. People who report problems are treated as troublemakers. Supervisors say they are “not team players.” Some workers lose good shifts after raising concerns. Others are ignored until they stop trying.

Eventually, most workers stay quiet unless a problem is impossible to hide. The official rule says “report concerns.” The lived rule says “keep quiet if you want to avoid trouble.”

Start with what you notice before trying to solve the whole problem.

For example, a quick judgment might blame one worker without noticing the pattern that trained silence.

3. Written Rule vs Lived Rule

Compare what is said with what is trained

The written rule and the lived rule can point in opposite directions. This section asks you to name both.

What does the official policy, handbook, instruction, or public statement say?

What do people learn is actually rewarded, punished, expected, or ignored?

Look for incentives, fear, silence, informal punishment, modeling, or repeated experience.

Name the larger result that begins to form.

4. Pattern Diagnosis

Use the Chapter 2 tool

Pattern Diagnosis

  1. Identify the repeated action.
  2. Identify the written rule.
  3. Identify the lived rule.
  4. Name the pattern.
  5. Identify who is affected.
  6. Locate the blockage.
  7. Propose pattern repair.

Concept reminder

A simple rule followed locally by many people can create a larger pattern no one individually wanted. Detecting a pattern does not erase responsibility. It helps locate where responsibility and repair need to happen.

Example: workers stay quiet, concerns are delayed, managers dismiss warnings, students avoid asking for help.

Give the pattern a short name, such as “silence around safety concerns” or “help-seeking gets punished.”

Include people who benefit, people who are burdened, people who are silenced, and people who inherit the risk.

Connect repeated actions, incentives, pressure, roles, expectations, or silence.

5. Suppression Gap / Structural Obstruction

Locate the blockage

Suppression gap

A concern can be seen, but people are discouraged, punished, ignored, or made unsafe when they try to raise it.

Structural obstruction

A problem can be seen, but the system does not provide a working way to fix it.

What makes this a suppression gap, a structural obstruction, both, or uncertain?

Optional comparison: Challenger warning route

A warning can fail when organizational roles, pressure, and burden-of-proof rules make it difficult for concern to travel into action. This does not mean no one is responsible. It means the warning route itself needs ethical attention.

6. Responsibility and Limits

Keep responsibility without oversimplifying

Important: Patterns shape action, but they do not make responsibility disappear. The question is where responsibility belongs and what kind of repair is possible.

Think about workers, supervisors, policies, leadership, processes, or the group as a whole.

Name roles or groups that could change incentives, routes, expectations, protection, or response.

Name what you do not know yet. This protects against pretending the map is complete.

7. Pattern Repair

Propose a repair route

Chapter 2 repair does not only ask one person to “try harder.” It asks what route, rule, incentive, or expectation must change so the better action becomes easier and safer.

For example: protected reporting, visible follow-up, no-retaliation enforcement, shared review, or a safer warning route.

Name a realistic first repair step, not a perfect solution.
